Building a Bowl, Building a Life: An Interview with ShaoChen, Founder of Eat Poke Helsinki

Background

Born and raised in Taiwan, ShaoChen Yang never envisioned himself running a poke restaurant in Helsinki. Food, however, had always been a part of his life: his first job was as a barista, where he managed small cafes around the city, and his earliest memories are of cooking with his grandmother, where he learned how to make traditional Taiwanese recipes such as Lu Rou Fan.

When ShaoChen moved to Finland in his twenties to study, he fell in love with the country’s clean ingredients and appreciation for simple, honest food. From this, the idea for Eat Poke was thus born.

What began as a small counter inside a market hall has now grown into a recognizable Helsinki brand. Eat Poke has now just opened their 10th chain in Helsinki, making it one of Helsinki’s biggest Poke chains.

Interview

Q1: What’s your story before starting Eat Poke, and what made you want to become an entrepreneur?

ShaoChen told me that the journey toward entrepreneurship wasn’t so much a single decision as it was a realization.

“I always loved food, but I never imagined opening a restaurant,” he said. “What actually pushed me to build my own restaurant was my culture, and the kind of food I missed.” A sentiment that rings true for many internationals that have relocated away from home. Eat Poke became that outlet, a space where he could merge Asian flavours from home with the high-quality Finnish produce.

Q2: What was the initial idea for your business?

“When I moved here, healthy food often meant salads: lettuce, a few toppings, and that was it,” ShaoChen explained. “I’m Asian, so a lot of our meals come with some kind of protein. That’s where the idea for Poke came in.”

The original idea behind Eat Poke was simply to bring a fresh, healthy and customizable experience to Helsinki. One that would provide a different experience to the salad bars that are so commonly found around the world.

Q3: Has your business idea changed over time? What is the current business model or mission?

“Honestly, the menu changed, but our priorities didn’t,” ShaoChen said.

He explained that Eat Poke grew organically. New toppings, new proteins, new sauces, many were inspired by Finnish customers’ curiosity or by ingredients he found while experimenting after hours.

“But we have always strived to provide fresh food, tasty flavours and to treat our customers kindly.”

Unlike most restaurants, Eat Poke didn’t rely heavily on marketing.

“Some customers have been with us since day one,” he noted. “They bring their families, they bring their coworkers. It means everything.”

Q4: What was the hardest part of starting your business?

“Everything except the cooking,” ShaoChen remarked.

He elaborated that rent, budgeting, and supply chain logistics were far more complicated and bothersome than his passion, developing recipes.

“Cooking for me is fun. Filling out paperwork in a language that isn’t yours... that’s a different challenge.”

But for him, the toughest part wasn’t just the technical aspects of running a business, it was also the emotional difficulty.

“There were moments where I felt very alone,” he shared. “When the fridge breaks at midnight, or when a supplier cancels, you realize: this is all on you.”

Still, he believes those challenges shaped him.

“I learned how to be resourceful, and I learned patience. You need to have a sort of ‘I’m going to figure it out’ mindset.”

Q5: How did you make Eat Poke stand out?

“I got lucky,” ShaoChen laughed.

He added that while he believes that Eat Poke provides quality food, fresh produce and great customer service, ShaoChen admits that his success is mostly due to his customers.

“We didn’t have a social media team at the start, and so a lot of our initial growth was based on word of mouth. Looking back, we were very lucky to have grown as we have.”

However, ShaoChen added that it wasn’t all pure luck. “People can taste when you care. Our sauces are homemade. Our toppings are prepared fresh every morning. We don’t freeze any of our food.” ShaoChen believes that it was this attention to detail that made Eat Poke stand out.

Q6: What are the most important lessons you've learned from starting your own business?

“The biggest lesson is to stay true to your purpose,” ShaoChen said.

He explained that, as Eat Poke grew, people encouraged him to expand aggressively, franchise, or compromise quality to reduce costs. But he refused.

“I wanted to make sure that I focused on maintaining standards before I expanded too quickly.” He stated, explaining that he wanted to ensure that they could maintain their quality before expanding. “That and it was too much work,” he said while laughing.

Another key lesson was humility.

“As an entrepreneur, you do every job,” he said. “You clean the tables, slice the fish, and take out the garbage. You can’t think anything is ‘below you.’” However, ShaoChen also stressed that this was a vital part of expanding. By taking on every role in the company, he learned how much time goes into each role, which allowed him to better estimate how many workers he needed to assign to each ‘job’.

Finally, ShaoChen emphasized perseverance.

“There will always be setbacks,” he said. “But if you believe in the vision, and you’re willing to work hard, things will move forward.”

Q7: If you could give yourself one piece of advice at the beginning of this journey, what would it be?

“Focus on making your customer happy, and everything else follows.”

ShaoChen explained that in the early days, Eat Poke never had a social media team. Instead, they relied on organic word of mouth to attract more customers.He explained that, in the early days, he doubted whether people would accept his food, whether his accent would be a barrier, or whether a small idea could survive in a competitive market.

But ShaoChen believes that his attention to detail and focus on customer experience were what ultimately set Eat Poke apart. As he put it, “make a business with care.”

Reflection and Takeaways

Interviewing ShaoChen reminded me that there isn’t necessarily a single formula to entrepreneurship. One’s path is shaped by culture, personal history, and the values a founder chooses to honor. ShaoChen never chased rapid scaling, flashy marketing, or aggressive expansion for its own sake. His and Eat Poke’s success came from consistency: staying true to his roots, never compromising on quality, and prioritizing customer satisfaction.

What stood out to me the most was how Eat Poke grew through authenticity rather than strategy. There were no big advertising campaigns, no venture-backed plans, just a focus on good food and customer service. Customers didn’t just buy bowls; they returned because they felt the care put into each one.

ShaoChen’s journey also reflects the dual nature of entrepreneurship. The practical side: rent, accounting, logistics, long nights fixing broken equipment. These are all unforeseen demands of the job that require discipline and resilience. But the emotional side is just as demanding: believing in your idea when no one else does, learning from mistakes, and persevering through moments of doubt.

In the end, Eat Poke’s success is not just about poke bowls; it’s about a founder who pursued his passion, always put customers first, and most importantly, never gave up. While some might call it luck, Eat Poke’s journey has taught me that luck can be created through hard work and relentless perseverance.
